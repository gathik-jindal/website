## Information

Intellectual goods or information is also a commodity that can be sold. It's very costly to produce and assemble, but the cost structure of an information supplier is very unusual. Since the very nature of competition in information markets is driven by this unusual cost structure due to how customers differ greatly in how they value particular information goods, we begin our overview of information strategy there.

### The cost of producing information

Information is costly to produce but cheap to reproduce. This leads to pricing based on the value to consumer rather than price to produce. The marginal costs of the product is so low that a 20% or 30% increase in the price makes no sense, so cost based pricing just doesn't work. Since people have varied sense of value to a particular information, value based pricing leads to naturally leads to differential pricing.

This can be done in multiple ways, once way publishers do this is by only selling the expensive hardback version of the book first and then the paper back versions. Providers of information on the internet can exploit the same strategy: investors now pay $8.95 a month for a Web site that offers portfolio analysis using 20- minute delayed stock market quotes but $50 a month for a service that uses real-time stock market quotes.

### Managing Intellectual Property

If the creators of an information good can reproduce it cheaply, others can copy it cheaply. It has long been recognized that some form of "privatization" of information helps to ensure its production. The U.S. Constitution explicitly grants Congress the duty "to promote the progress of science and useful arts, by securing, for limited times, to authors and inventors, the exclusive right to their respective writings and discoveries.

But the legal grant of exclusive rights it not enough, with the rise of technology piracy is now easier than ever. If copies crowd out legitimate sales, the producers of information may not be able to recover their productions costs.

### Information as an "Experience Good"

Economists say that a good is an experience good if consumers must consume it to value it. That makes information an experience good every time its consumed. There are various strategies to get wary customers to overcome their reluctance to purchase information before they know what they're getting, through providing attractive samples to building a brand reputation.

>The tension between giving away your information—to let people know what you have to offer—and charging them for it to recover your costs is a fundamental problem in the information economy.

### The Economies of Attention

> A wealth of information creates a poverty of attention

Nowadays the problem is not information access but information overload. The real value produced by an information provider comes in locating, filtering, and communicating what is useful to the consumer. It is no accident that the most popular Web sites belong to the search engines, those devices that allow people to find information they value and to avoid the rest

To popularize your goods or service, you need to let people know about it. This is called advertising.

Amazon.com once entered an agreement with AOL to gain access to AOL's 8.5 million customers. The cost of this deal in on the order of 19 million dollars, which can be understood as the cost of purchasing the attention of AOL's subscribers.

Wal-Mart had launched a television network, which broadcasts commercials on the televisions sets lined up for sale at the company's 1.950 stores nation wide. Like AOL, Wal-Mart realized that it could sell the attention of its customers to advertisers. As health clubs, doctor's offices, and other locations attempt to grab our valuable attention, information overload will worsen. (it has)

Advertising works because it exploits statistical patterns. People who read Car and Driver are likely to be interested in ads for BMWs, and people who read the Los Angeles Times are likely to be interested in California real estate.

The internet, a hybrid between a broadcast medium and a point to point medium, offers exciting new potentials for matching up customers and suppliers. TV Show directors collect viewing habits of their customers from one season to designs TV shows for the next season. In contrast, Web Servers can observe the behavior of thousands of  customers and immediately produce personalized content, bundled with customized ads.

Not just behavior but they can amass their's users history and demographics, hotmail once provided free e-mail service to users who completed a questionnaire on their demographics and interests.

### System Competition

>Focus not just on your competitors but also on your collaborators and complementors.

Usually, one firm cannot hope to offer all the pieces that make up an information system. Instead, different components are made by different manufacturers using very different production and business models. Traditional rules of competitive strategy focus on competitors, suppliers, and customers. In the information economy, companies selling complementary components, or complementors, are equally important. When you are selling one component of a system, you can't compete if you're not compatible with the rest of the system.

In the area of infotech this is more important than ever. Microsoft succeeded because of its partnership with Intel, Apple choose to go with producing both. But they didn't last due to lack of competition.

### Lock-In and Switching Costs

When products or services depend on complementary products and services they are in "Lock in", they are vulnerable to changes made by the the product or service they depend on, unless the switching costs are low as compared. This is not restricted to just products but also to users and brands.

It is important to identify these Lock-Ins and their switching costs, not just yours but also the ones that are locked-in on you.

Lock-in is not absolute - new technologies do displace old ones - but switching costs can dramatically alter firm's strategies and options. In fact, the magnitude of switching costs is itself a strategic choice made by the producer of the system.

Lock-in arises whenever users invest in multiple complementary and durable assets specific to a particular information technology system

### Positive Feedback, Network Externalities, and Standards

For many information technologies, consumers benefit from using a popular format or system. When the value of a product to one user depends on how many other users there are, economists say that this product exhibits network externalities, or network effects.

Technologies subject to strong network effects tend to exhibit long lead times followed by explosive growth. The pattern results from positive feedback: as the installed base of users grows, more and more users find adoption worthwhile. The company that best understands information systems and complementary products will be best positioned to move rapidly and aggressively.

>In competing to become the standard, or at least to achieve critical mass, consumer expectations are critical. In a very real sense, the product that is expected to become the standard will become the standard. Self-fulfilling expectations are one manifestation of positive-feedback economics and 'bandwagon effects. As a result, companies participating in markets with strong network effects seek to convince customers that their products will ultimately become the standard, while rival, incompatible products will soon be orphaned.

Because of the importance of critical mass, because customer expectations are so important in the area of information infrastructure, and because technology is evolving so rapidly, the timing of strategic moves is even more important in the information industry than in others. Moving too early means making compromises in technology and going out on a limb without sufficient allies.

Whether you are trying to establish a new information technology or to extend the lifetime of technology that is already popular, you will face critical compatibility decisions.

Another method for achieving critical mass is to assemble a powerful group of strategic partners. For this purpose, partners can be customers, complementers, or even competitors. Having some large, visible customers aboard can get the bandwagon rolling by directly building up critical mass.

>Having suppliers of complements aboard makes the overall system more attractive. And having competitors aboard can give today's and tomorrow's customers the assurance that they will not be exploited once they are locked in.

>If you own valuable intellectual property but need to gain critical mass, you must decide whether to promote your technology unilaterally, in the hope that it will become a de facto standard that you can tightly control, or to rfiake various "openness" commitments to help achieve a critical mass.
